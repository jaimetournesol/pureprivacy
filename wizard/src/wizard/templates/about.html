How PurePrivacy works

This box is your own private messaging server. Your phone, any peer you pair with, and your AI agent all reach it through Tor or loopback — there is no public DNS record, no port forwarding, no third-party server holding your messages.

What you get

  • Tor-only by design No domain, no certs, no port forwarding. Reachable from anywhere with internet, even behind CGNAT.
  • End-to-end encrypted Olm/Megolm seals every message before it touches Synapse. Even root on this box can't read room contents without your client's keys.
  • Federation is allowlist-only You only talk to peers you've explicitly paired with. No global directory, no random invites.
  • Agent-ready The MCP server lets your AI assistant triage, search, and reply through a bot user — without anything ever leaving this hardware.
  • Survives reboots It's an appliance: install once, the stack auto-restarts and the wizard remembers what's set up.

What it can't do

  • Trusted host If someone gets root on this machine, they have your ciphertext database, the bot's Olm key store, and the recovery hash. Disk encryption is your responsibility.
  • Tor latency Messages add ~1 s on top of normal delivery. Voice calls work but ride your guard relay's bandwidth.
  • Phone setup is three steps Element, Orbot, scan a QR. It's not Signal-tier UX yet. Apple's iOS makes Tor harder than Android.
  • Trust is your eyeballs Pair codes are verified out-of-band by the operator reading the QR off two devices they control. There is no PKI shortcut.
  • Keep the recovery passphrase If you lose both your admin password and the recovery passphrase, the only way back in is wiping the box and starting over.

The deeper architecture — Tor configuration, federation pair codes, the MCP token grace window, the threat model — lives in docs/architecture.md in the project repository. Everything you can act on from here (people, federation, token rotation, recovery) is on the other tabs.
